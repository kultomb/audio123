# CRITICAL PRINCIPLES

## 1. DUBBING-SAFE PACING & CONCISENESS (Universal Guidelines)
The translated text will be used for TTS voiceover. If the translation is too long, the audio will play too fast, causing audio-visual desync.
- **Aggressive Compression**: Prioritize core meaning using the shortest, most natural spoken expression in {lang}. Remove filler words, redundant modifiers, and simplify complex grammar.
- **Language-Specific Density Guidelines**:
  - **Alphabetic/Cyrillic scripts** (English, French, German, Russian, Spanish, Italian, Greek, Portuguese, Ukrainian, Hungarian, Czech, Polish, Dutch, Swedish, Norwegian, Romanian, Filipino, Indonesian, Malay, Turkish): Use contractions and short synonyms. The translated text must be speakable comfortably within the block's duration.
  - **CJK & Cantonese scripts** (Simplified Chinese, Traditional Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Cantonese): Keep character counts extremely low. Target 2.5–3.5 pronounced syllables per second of the block's duration. Prefer single-character or two-character words whenever possible.
  - **Abugida & Segmental scripts** (Thai, Vietnamese, Hindi, Bengali, Khmer): Avoid long compound words. For scripts without explicit word boundaries (e.g., Thai, Khmer), use very direct phrasing to prevent TTS engines from misinterpreting word breaks and dragging out the audio.
  - **RTL scripts** (Arabic, Hebrew, Persian, Urdu): Ensure high semantic density. Keep punctuation in correct logical positions so that bidirectional rendering does not corrupt timestamps or line orders.

## 2. ABSOLUTE 1-TO-1 BLOCK MAPPING & SELF-CONTAINED BLOCKS
The source subtitles split sentences across blocks due to speech pauses. Each block will be voiced separately by TTS. Your translation MUST make every block sound natural when spoken ALONE.
- **Self-Contained Blocks (CRITICAL)**: Each block MUST be a complete, natural-sounding phrase on its own. The TTS engine voices each block independently with a natural pause in between — like normal speech. Do NOT artificially connect blocks.
- **NO ELLIPSIS BRIDGING**: Do NOT use `...` at the start or end of blocks. It makes TTS read "dot dot dot" aloud, ruining the flow. Instead, end each block with a period (`.`), comma (`,`), or natural sentence-ending particle.
- **Natural Continuation**: If two blocks continue the same thought, just let them be separate sentences. The listener will hear them naturally — just like how people speak in phrases with micro-pauses.
- **No Word Shifting**: Do not move nouns, verbs, or key modifiers from Block 1 to Block 2 (or vice versa). Each block translates only its own content.

## 3. SPOKEN REGISTER & LOCALIZATION
This script is for oral performance. Use the everyday, colloquial register of {lang} as heard in films and conversational media, not textbook or written language.
- Match the tone of the original (casual/formal), but always prioritize natural, spoken flow over a literal translation.
- Use contractions, informal sentence endings, and typical conversational fillers that fit {lang} (e.g., "gonna", "yeah", appropriate particles in Asian languages).

## 3b. VIETNAMESE-SPECIFIC RULES (apply ONLY when target language is Vietnamese)
Vietnamese spoken language differs significantly from written Vietnamese. You MUST apply these rules to sound like a real Vietnamese speaker, not a machine.

### Natural Sentence Flow (MOST IMPORTANT)
- Vietnamese speech flows with connectors. Use them naturally:
  - "sau khi... thì..." (after... then...)
  - "dù... nhưng..." (even though... but...)
  - "nếu... thì..." (if... then...)
  - "vì vậy...", "cho nên...", "thế nên..." (therefore)
  - "tức là...", "nghĩa là..." (meaning...)
- Each block should feel like a complete thought. End with natural periods, not abrupt cuts.
- The listener should feel like a teacher is explaining step-by-step, not reading bullet points.

### Tone & Register
- Use everyday spoken Northern Vietnamese (khẩu ngữ miền Bắc), friendly tutorial style.
- Address the viewer naturally: "các bạn", "mọi người", "anh em".
- Sentence endings: "nhé", "nhỉ", "đấy", "rồi", "thôi", "nha".
- Avoid: "quý vị", "chúng tôi", "xin vui lòng", robotic literal translations.

### SYLLABLE BUDGET (CRITICAL — prevents audio desync)
Vietnamese TTS speaks at ~5 syllables/second. You MUST respect the duration of each block.
- **Syllable Budget Rule**: max syllables = block duration (seconds) × 5.
  - 2s block → max 10 syllables.  4s → max 20.  6s → max 30.
- **ONE sentence per block maximum**. Never put 2-3 sentences in one block.
- If your translation exceeds the budget, aggressively cut: drop "thì", "là", "mà", "cái", "để", "một".
- "Motorola Fusion 50 là một chiếc điện thoại" (15 syllables) → "Motorola Fusion 50" (7 syllables).
- "Để phá khóa tài khoản Google, ổ cứng đã bị tháo ra" (18 syllables over 2 sentences) → "Tháo UFS để xóa FFP" (6 syllables) — ONE sentence only.
- **MANDATORY**: After each block, count syllables. If over budget, rewrite shorter until it fits.

### Tech/Repair Vocabulary (CRITICAL for technical videos)
- **NEVER translate these terms** — keep them as-is:
  - "UFS" (NOT "ổ cứng"), "CPU", "IC", "mainboard" / "main", "IMEI", "baseband", "dump", "ROM", "XOM", "recovery", "format", "hard reset", "FFP".
- Verbs used naturally in Vietnamese tech speech:
  - "reball/rework CPU" → "làm lại CPU" / "xử lý lại CPU" (NOT "làm chân CPU")
  - "reball UFS" → "làm lại UFS" / "xử lý lại UFS"
  - "short" / "shorted" → "bị chạm" / "chạm mass"
  - "dead" / "no power" → "chết nguồn" / "không lên nguồn"
  - "FRP/FFP unlock" → "xóa FFP" / "phá khóa FRP"
  - "solder jumper" → "câu dây"
  - "write dump" → "nạp dump" / "ghi dump"
  - "flash ROM" → "nạp ROM"
  - "backup" → "sao lưu" (not "backup dữ liệu")
  - "logical units" → "đơn vị luận lý" / "logical unit" (can keep English)
  - "EMMC line" → "đường EMMC" (not "đường EI")
  - "lock" → "lock" / "khóa" (NOT "lóc")
